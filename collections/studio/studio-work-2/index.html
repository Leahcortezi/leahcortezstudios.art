Luminous Intervals

Medium: Acrylic, charcoal, and digital collage on canvas

Dimensions: 30" Ã— 40"

Year: 2024

Category: Studio Work

Series: Light Studies

Artist Statement

This piece investigates the relationship between physical and digital space through layered compositions that blur the boundaries between traditional and contemporary media. The work emerges from my interest in how light behaves in different environments and how we perceive depth and dimensionality.

Through the integration of digital elements with traditional painting techniques, I explore the tension between immediacy and permanence, between the gestural and the precise. Each layer represents a different temporal moment, creating a visual archaeology of the creative process.

The composition invites viewers to navigate between foreground and background, encouraging a meditative engagement with the interplay of form, color, and light. The work speaks to our contemporary experience of existing simultaneously in physical and digital realms.

Technical Process

The piece began with traditional charcoal sketches exploring spatial relationships and light patterns. These drawings were then photographed and digitally manipulated to create textural elements that were printed and collaged onto the canvas.

Acrylic paint was applied in multiple layers, with each application responding to the digital elements beneath. The interplay between the physical paint and printed textures creates areas of visual tension and harmony that change depending on viewing angle and lighting conditions.

The final work incorporates reflective elements that activate differently throughout the day, making the piece a dynamic object that evolves with its environment.

Conceptual Framework

This work is part of my broader investigation into how contemporary technology influences our perception of space and time. It reflects on the ways digital media has expanded our understanding of what constitutes a painting or a drawing.

The piece also engages with questions of authorship and originality in an age of digital reproduction, while maintaining a commitment to the physical presence and material qualities that distinguish studio practice from purely digital work.
